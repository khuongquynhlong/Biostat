Questions¶

Fit two multiple linear regression models with the bird species richness as outcome variable.

  1. For model 1, consider the following regressors: AET, PET, PET2 and AET2. You do not have to include interaction effects. Assess the model assumptions and report the results from this data analysis.
  1. For model 2, consider the following regressors: AET, PET and PETdiscrete. Include the interaction effect of PET and PETdiscrete. Give an interpretation of the estimated model parameters. Does this model fit well? Make a prediction of the species richness for an area with PET = 400 mm / year and AET = 100 mm /year.

All tests must be performed at the 5% level of significance, and confidence and/or prediction intervals must have a coverage level of 95%.

All data analysis must be performed with SAS

Model defintion¶

The Model 1 can be writen as:

$$\textrm{Bird species richness}_i|\textrm{AET, PET } i.i.d. \sim N(\beta_0 + \beta_1AET_i + \beta_2AET_i^2 + \beta_3PET_i + \beta_4PET_i^2, \sigma^2)$$

$$\textrm{Bird species richness}_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1AET_i + \beta_2AET_i^2 + \beta_3PET_i + \beta_4PET_i^2 + \varepsilon _i$$

With $\varepsilon_i i.i.d. N(0, \sigma^2)$

Figure 3: Model assumptions diagnosis plots for Model 1

Model assumptions

  • Linearity of the regression model: Based on residual vs. regressor plots, we can see the average residuals have no systematic pattern as a function of the regressor. Thus, we can conclude that the linearity assumption is met.
  • Homoskedasticity: Similarly, there are no clear pattern of the variation of residual vs. regressor (except for some data points in the tail of AET), which suggests that the constant-variance assumption is satisfied.
  • Normality of the error term: Based on QQ-plot of the residuals, the normality assumption does hold since there are no systematic deviations from the diagonal line

🠆 Thus, the assumptions for Model 1 are satisfied

Note: Regarding multicollinearity, although the vif are high for parameters, it is expected since the model includes the quadratic terms.

Interpret the parameter estimates¶

The model 1 can be writen as

$$\textrm{Bird species richness}_i = -377.75 + 0.91939AET_i - 0.00177AET_i^2 + 1.74979PET_i - 0.00135PET_i^2 + \varepsilon _i$$

With $\varepsilon_i i.i.d. N(0, \sigma^2)$


  • Conditional on PET, the average change of bird species richness when AET inceases by C units is:
$$0.91939(AET + C) - 0.00177(AET + C)^2 - 0.91939AET + 0.00177AET^2 = 0.91939C - 0.00177C^2 - 0.00354C*AET$$
  • Similarly, conditional on AET, the average change of bird species richness when PET inceases by C units is:
$$1.74979(PET + C) - 0.00135(PET + C)^2 - 1.74979PET + 0.00135PET^2 = 1.74979C - 0.00135C^2 - 0.0027C*PET$$

The results are statistically significant at p < 0.001

Interpret the parameter estimates¶

The model 2 can be writen as

$$\textrm{Bird species richness}_i = -226.99 + 0.24945AET_i + 0.96581PET_i + 529.20PET_{discrete_i} - 1.10874PET_i*PET_{discrete_i} + \varepsilon _i$$

With $\varepsilon_i i.i.d. N(0, \sigma^2)$

  • Among places with low energy (PETdiscrete = 0): conditional on AET, each unit increases in PET is associated with an average of 0.96581 unit increase in bird species richness
  • Among places with high energy (PETdiscrete = 1): conditional on AET each unit increases in PET is associated with an average of (0.96581 - 1.10874) unit increase in bird species richness

🠆 In conclusion: conditional on AET, the effect of PET on bird species richness (slope of PET) depends on the level of energy (low/high). Specifically, the effect of PET on bird species richness (slope of PET) in high energy areas is lower by 1.10874, as compared to areas with low energy. The 95% confidence interval from 0.86483 to 1.35264, p-value < 0.001.
